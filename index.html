The prototype

Tap "Save on your bill" to start the guided tour. The chart highlights each pricing window with live annotations pointing at your usage data. The tour ends with 3 suggested timing shifts. Tap "Do it" on anything that feels feasible and the saving is revealed as a reward after committing.

Note on AI: All AI behaviour is simulated and no real model responses are generated. In a real product, AI would recognise individual usage patterns, generate personalised coaching copy per household profile and rank suggestions by feasibility for that specific user. That last capability, inferring which suggestions are actually actionable for a given user, is the hardest and most valuable part and a testable hypothesis for the canary cohort.

Hi Pavel, and the team reviewing this

Below is my submission for the product discovery case. The interactive prototype is above. I built this over two days, one day of research and problem definition and one day of prototyping. I used AI tooling throughout, specifically Claude and ChatGPT, both to compress research time and to build the prototype. Background, process and design rationale are documented below.

Process

I treated this as a two-day discovery sprint. The diagram below shows the standardised process I try to follow in discovery work. This submission maps directly to it.

My discovery process board, from task-in to handover. Steps without a bold border (e.g. "Decision criteria, align with PM" and "Target group interviews") were not completed for this submission but are standard steps in my normal process inside an organisation.

1. Time-plan. Scoped the problem before touching any tooling and grounded the work in a clear question before moving forward.

2. Eliq research. Read the website, product positioning and mission carefully. Key anchors were "When energy data is made understandable, customers stay involved" and "offering energy efficiency and optimisation as a service." Eliq operates a B2B2C model where the paying customer is a utility and the end user is a homeowner. Any solution needs to work at both levels.

3. Customer research. Used ChatGPT as a structured market research tool with the following prompt: "You are a market researcher for the electricity and utilities sector. Consumers report confusion over Time-of-Use tariffs. Do market research on what is typically confusing. Do a customer segmentation and apply root cause analysis to each group. Focus on the Nordic, British, Baltic, and European market overall." Design decisions were anchored to these outputs.

4. Key segmentation finding. The highest-impact segment is busy homeowners, people who don't have time to actively optimise their energy routines but could make small timing shifts if prompted in the right way. This segment offers both a behaviour change opportunity and an education opportunity, with behaviour change as the ceiling and education as the floor.

5. Critical research finding. One finding from the literature directly shaped the prototype design. "Making marginal per-unit prices salient significantly increases WTA estimates compared to when per-unit prices are merely implied." Consumer Acceptance of Time-of-Use Tariffs: The Role of Information and Price Salience, ESRI (2024). Read paper → In practice this means: the more explicit the saving shown upfront, the more compensation users implicitly demand before changing behaviour. Based on this, specific savings are withheld until after the user commits and then revealed as a reward. The hook uses aggregate social-norm framing rather than personal projections.

Reframing the problem from educational to behavioural

The case prompt centres on a gap in understanding, with 60% of homeowners reporting they don't understand ToU tariffs. Education is absolutely part of the answer and the prototype is designed to deliver it. But understanding alone rarely changes habits. Research on energy feedback systems consistently shows that people can grasp a concept without acting on it.

The design hypothesis was to lead with personal relevance rather than tariff mechanics and let the education happen through the user's own data rather than through explanation. This guided several concrete decisions. The entry point became "Save on your bill" rather than "ToU guide". The tour points at the user's own chart rather than explaining pricing in the abstract. Suggestions are framed as feasibility questions rather than financial calculations.

How might we help busy homeowners understand the impact of their energy timing and make it easy enough to act on that understanding right now?

Why this fits Eliq

Eliq's stated mission is to help utilities transform from commodity suppliers into digital businesses offering energy efficiency as a service. The gap this prototype addresses, between a customer who sees their usage and one who changes it, is exactly where that value lives. A utility that can show measurable peak-shifting has a stronger case for demand-side flexibility programmes and a more direct response to regulatory pressure on consumer energy literacy across European markets.

Smart meter prevalence matters here. Sweden and the UK are among the furthest ahead in Eliq's markets, with the UK government programme driving near-universal rollout. The prototype's personalised pattern recognition depends on disaggregated appliance data being available, making it a feature that grows more valuable as smart meter penetration increases across other markets.

Measuring success

Three KPIs ordered by how quickly they return signal, running from intent through early behavioural evidence to sustained outcome.

KPI 1: commit rate. Of users who complete the guide, what percentage tap "Do it" on at least one suggestion? Feasibility-driven commits are a stronger proxy for follow-through than financially-motivated ones and serve as the primary optimisation metric for guide iteration.

KPI 2: time to first action. Hours between commitment and first detected behaviour change. A short lag confirms the commitment was genuine. A long lag is early warning that KPI 3 will underperform, giving weeks of signal before the outcome window closes. Requires appliance-level data. Target: median under 48 hours.

KPI 3: sustained behaviour change. Sustained reduction in peak-to-offpeak ratio (10% or more) over 6 to 8 weeks compared to a matched control group from the same canary cohort. The control group is non-negotiable. Target: statistically significant delta at 8 weeks.
